Matai Tech - Objection Handling Guide

==================================================
HOW TO USE THIS GUIDE
==================================================

This document provides detailed responses to common objections and concerns.

Format for each objection:
- The objection (what the prospect says)
- Why they're saying it (underlying concern)
- How to respond (strategic approach)
- Specific language to use

==================================================
PRICING OBJECTIONS
==================================================

OBJECTION: "That's too expensive."
----------------------------------------------
Underlying concern: Sticker shock, haven't calculated current cost

Response strategy: Reframe as investment with ROI math

What to say:
"I understand the setup fee feels significant. Let's look at what you're currently spending on manual work.

You mentioned your team spends 20 hours per week on [task]. At a fully-loaded cost of $30/hour, that's $600/week or $31,200 per year.

Our Efficiency Package is $3,500 + $350/month = $7,700 first year.

If we automate that task and save even 70% of that time, you're saving $21,840/year, netting $14,140 in year one.

That's not expensive - that's a 2.8x return on investment. The real question is: can you afford NOT to automate?"

Follow-up questions:
- "What's your current cost of doing this manually?"
- "What's the opportunity cost of your team's time on this vs. strategic work?"
- "What would you do with 20 extra hours per week?"


OBJECTION: "We don't have budget right now."
----------------------------------------------
Underlying concern: Budget not allocated, need to justify to others

Response strategy: Help them make the business case internally

What to say:
"I totally understand. Let's figure out if this makes sense to prioritize in your budget.

Here's a simple way to think about it: You're already spending money on this problem in the form of labor cost. We're just reallocating some of that existing spend to a solution that reduces the ongoing cost.

For example: If you're spending $2,000/month in labor on manual work, and our solution costs $350/month but saves you $1,500/month, you're net positive $1,150/month immediately after the setup period.

Would it help if I put together a one-page ROI analysis you can use to get budget approval?"

Alternative approaches:
- "When does your next budget cycle start? Let's plan ahead."
- "Could we start with the Discovery Sprint ($1,500) to prove ROI before requesting full budget?"
- "Would a payment plan for the setup fee help? We can split it over 2-3 months."


OBJECTION: "Can you do it cheaper?"
----------------------------------------------
Underlying concern: Testing if there's negotiation room, or truly budget-constrained

Response strategy: Explain value, don't discount arbitrarily

What to say:
"Our pricing is based on the ROI we deliver, not arbitrary numbers. Here's why our packages are priced as they are:

1. We guarantee 30-day ROI or refund your setup fee
2. We build for 99%+ reliability (most freelancers build to 70-80%)
3. Ongoing support and optimization included
4. You own everything - no vendor lock-in

If we reduce the price, we'd need to reduce scope or quality. That defeats the purpose - a cheap automation that breaks is more expensive than no automation.

That said, if budget is truly constrained, here are options:
1. Start with Discovery Sprint ($1,500) - prove ROI, then expand
2. Choose Efficiency Package (smaller scope but still valuable)
3. We can discuss payment plans for the setup fee

What aspect of the package would you want to reduce to lower cost?"

What NOT to say:
- "Ok, I can do 20% off" (devalues your service)
- "How much can you afford?" (race to the bottom)


OBJECTION: "Your competitor quoted half this price."
----------------------------------------------
Underlying concern: Wants to ensure they're getting fair value

Response strategy: Differentiate on value and reliability, not price

What to say:
"That's an important comparison to make. Let me explain what you get for the difference:

Our competitor likely charges:
- Hourly rates with uncertain total costs
- No ROI guarantee
- Limited ongoing support after delivery
- May not include error handling and monitoring
- May disappear after project ends

We provide:
- Fixed pricing (you know total cost upfront)
- 30-day ROI guarantee (refund if we don't deliver value)
- Ongoing monitoring and support included
- 99%+ uptime commitment
- Long-term partnership approach

The question isn't 'who's cheapest?' but 'who will deliver the most value?'

A $3,500 automation that saves you $20,000/year is vastly better than a $1,500 automation that breaks constantly and saves you nothing.

I'd encourage you to ask your competitor:
- What's their uptime guarantee?
- What happens if it breaks after delivery?
- What's included in ongoing support?
- What's their refund policy if it doesn't deliver ROI?

Then compare apples to apples."


OBJECTION: "We can probably do this ourselves with Zapier."
----------------------------------------------
Underlying concern: Wondering if they need professional help

Response strategy: Acknowledge DIY is valid for simple cases, differentiate on complexity

What to say:
"You absolutely might be able to! Zapier is great for simple workflows, and I always encourage clients to DIY when it makes sense.

Where we add value:
1. Complex workflows (10+ steps with branching logic)
2. Error handling and edge cases (what happens when APIs fail?)
3. Reliability (we build for 99%+ uptime, most DIY is 70-80%)
4. Time (we can build in days what might take you weeks to figure out)
5. Ongoing monitoring and optimization

Questions to consider:
- How much time will it take you to build and test this?
- What happens when it breaks at 2am?
- Who maintains it when you're on vacation?
- Can you handle all the edge cases?

If your workflows are simple (2-3 steps, low volume), DIY makes total sense.

If they're complex or mission-critical, it's like home renovation - you CAN do it yourself, but hiring a professional saves time, reduces mistakes, and ensures it's done right.

Want to schedule a discovery call to assess which approach makes sense for your situation?"

   - Instant alerts if something fails

3. Fast response:
   - Critical issues: 4-hour response time

"What specific scenario are you concerned about? I can explain how we'd handle that."

----------------------------------------------
Underlying concern: Don't have time for implementation

Response strategy: Explain low time commitment, highlight that being busy is exactly why they need automation

What to say:
"I totally get it - being too busy is exactly why you need automation!

Here's the irony: You're too busy with manual work to implement the automation that would make you less busy. It's a catch-22.

The good news is, our process requires minimal time from you:
- Week 1: 2-3 hours for discovery conversations
- Weeks 2-4: 30 min/week for check-ins
- Total commitment: 5-8 hours spread over a month

Compare that to the 20+ hours per week you'll save ongoing.

Think of it like going to the gym. You're 'too busy' for a 30-minute workout, but that workout would give you more energy and capacity for everything else.

That said, if now really isn't the time, I understand. When would your schedule open up?"

Follow-up:
"What if we handled most work async (via email/Slack) to minimize disruption to your schedule?"

TECHNICAL OBJECTIONS
==================================================

OBJECTION: "Our systems don't have APIs." / "We use legacy software."
----------------------------------------------
Underlying concern: Technical feasibility

Response strategy: Explore workarounds, be honest about limitations

What to say:
"Let's figure out what's possible. Legacy systems can be challenging, but we have options:

Option 1: File-based integration
- Export data from your system to CSV/Excel
- Automated pickup and processing
- Import processed data back
- Not real-time, but often good enough

Option 2: Database direct access
- Many legacy systems use SQL databases we can connect to
- Read/write directly to database
- Requires database permissions

Option 3: Web scraping / RPA
- Automate the user interface
- More fragile, requires more maintenance
- Last resort option

Option 4: Hybrid approach
- Automate what we can
- Manual handoff for legacy system portions
- Still saves significant time

The honest answer is: Some systems are too difficult or risky to automate. If that's the case, I'll tell you upfront rather than take your money for something that won't work.

Can you tell me specifically which systems you use? I'll assess feasibility."


OBJECTION: "We have complex/custom requirements."
----------------------------------------------
Underlying concern: Will off-the-shelf solution work for us?

Response strategy: Explain customization capabilities, assess fit

What to say:
"Complex requirements are actually our specialty - that's why clients hire us instead of using Zapier themselves.

Our approach:
1. Understand your specific requirements in detail
2. Map to standard patterns where possible
3. Custom logic for unique aspects
4. Build with flexibility for edge cases

Examples of 'complex' requirements we've handled:
- Multi-step approval workflows with dynamic routing
- Custom calculations and business logic
- Integration between 6+ systems
- Handling 20+ different scenarios based on conditions

That said, some things are too complex or truly need custom software. If we get to that point, I'll tell you.

Can you describe your specific requirements? I'll let you know if we can handle it."


OBJECTION: "We need this to work with [obscure tool]."
----------------------------------------------
Underlying concern: Integration availability

Response strategy: Research tool, explain options

What to say:
"Let me look into [tool] and see what's available.

[Research: Does it have an API? Existing integrations on Make/Zapier/n8n?]

Option 1: Direct integration exists
'Great news - [tool] has existing integration on [platform]. We can connect directly.'

Option 2: API available, no pre-built integration
'[Tool] has an API. We'll need to build a custom integration using HTTP requests, but it's totally doable. May add 1-2 days to timeline.'

Option 3: No API
'Unfortunately [tool] doesn't have an API. Options:
- Export/import via files
- Consider migrating to alternative tool with better integration
- Manual handoff for this portion of workflow

Would you be open to exploring alternative tools if it makes automation much easier?'

The key is I'll never tell you it works if it doesn't. I'd rather be honest than set false expectations."

VALUE AND SCOPE OBJECTIONS
==================================================

OBJECTION: "Can't you just automate everything in our business?"
----------------------------------------------
Underlying concern: Unrealistic expectations about scope

Response strategy: Set realistic expectations, focus on high-impact

What to say:
"I love the ambition! But let me give you realistic expectations:

Not everything should be automated:
- Tasks requiring human judgment or creativity
- Exception-heavy processes
- Tasks done infrequently (not worth automation)
- Processes that are still changing

What we focus on:
- Rule-based, repetitive tasks
- High-volume operations
- Time-consuming manual work
- Integration between disconnected systems

Our approach is the 80/20 rule:
- Identify the 20% of tasks consuming 80% of time
- Automate those high-impact processes first
- Deliver measurable ROI quickly
- Expand over time as we find more opportunities

Typical first implementation:
- 2-5 core automations
- Targeting the biggest time drains
- Saving 10-20 hours per week
- Once those are stable and delivering value, we expand

Starting with 'automate everything' usually fails. Starting with high-impact wins builds confidence and momentum."

OBJECTION: "This seems like just basic integration - why can't we do it ourselves?"
----------------------------------------------
Underlying concern: Questioning value of professional help

Response strategy: Acknowledge simplicity at surface, explain depth

What to say:
"You're right that the concept is simple - connect system A to system B. Here's where the complexity comes in:

1. Error handling:
   - What happens when API is down?
   - What if data format changes?
   - How to prevent duplicate records?
   - How to recover from partial failures?

2. Edge cases:
   - What if customer has 2 emails?
   - What if phone number format is wrong?
   - What if required field is empty?
   - What if timezone differences cause issues?

3. Reliability:
   - DIY automations typically run at 70-80% success rate
   - Professional automations run at 99%+
   - That 20% difference is everything

4. Maintenance:
   - APIs change
   - Platforms update
   - Who fixes it when you're on vacation?

5. Scaling:
   - Works fine with 10 records/day
   - Breaks at 100 records/day
   - Need to architect for scale

The basic integration might take you a weekend. The error handling, edge cases, monitoring, and maintenance take professional experience.

It's like saying 'Why hire a plumber? I can just connect two pipes.' Sure, but will it leak? Will it meet code? Will it work reliably for years?

That said, if your needs are truly simple, DIY might work fine. Want to discuss your specific use case?"

   - We train your whole team
   - Clear documentation
   - Ongoing support as they adapt

4. Incremental rollout:
   - Start with one automation
   - Build confidence
   - Expand gradually

5. Make it easy:
   - Design intuitive interfaces
   - Minimize behavior change required
   - Automation works in background when possible

The key message to your team:
'This automation eliminates the boring, repetitive work so you can focus on the parts of your job that actually require your skills and judgment.'

Want me to join your team meeting to address concerns directly?"

HOW TO HANDLE OBJECTIONS GENERALLY
==================================================

FRAMEWORK: LARA (Listen, Acknowledge, Respond, Ask)

1. LISTEN:
   - Let them fully express the objection
   - Don't interrupt or get defensive
   - Understand the underlying concern

2. ACKNOWLEDGE:
   - "That's a valid concern."
   - "I understand where you're coming from."
   - "That's a smart question to ask."
   - Show respect for their concern

3. RESPOND:
   - Address the specific concern
   - Provide evidence (numbers, case studies, guarantees)
   - Offer solutions or alternatives
   - Be honest about limitations

4. ASK:
   - "Does that address your concern?"
   - "What else would you need to know?"
   - "What questions do you have?"
   - Move conversation forward

WHAT NOT TO DO:
- Get defensive ("You're wrong to think that")
- Dismiss concerns ("That's not a real problem")
- Pressure ("You need to decide now")
- Lie or exaggerate ("We can automate anything")

==================================================
WHEN TO WALK AWAY
==================================================

Some objections indicate poor fit:

RED FLAGS:
- "We can't afford this" + clear lack of budget
- "We change processes constantly" = not ready
- "We need this done in 2 days" = unrealistic expectations
- "Can you do it for half price?" after detailed explanation
- "We'll only pay if it works" = unreasonable risk transfer

In these cases, politely decline:
"Based on our conversation, I don't think we're the right fit for your situation right now. Here's what I'd recommend instead: [alternative]. Happy to revisit in the future when timing is better."

==================================================

Remember: Objections are often a sign of interest, not rejection. They're asking questions because they're seriously considering it. Address concerns honestly and thoroughly, and many objections turn into closed deals.
